# Analysis Frameworks

Apply these using REAL data from discovery and research. Every data point must trace back to a source.

## A. Market Gap Analysis

For the researched location:
1. List every business type present within the research radius (from competitor data)
2. Identify categories with ZERO or LOW competition
3. Cross-reference with demand signals — is there demand for what's missing?
4. Identify underserved demographics (e.g., "families everywhere but no kids' activities")
5. Output as a table: Category | Present | Competition Level | Demand Evidence

## B. Porter's Five Forces

For each top candidate, in plain language (not MBA jargon):
- **New entrants**: How easy for someone else to start the same thing here?
- **Supplier power**: Inputs available locally? Multiple suppliers or few?
- **Buyer power**: How price-sensitive? Do they have alternatives?
- **Substitutes**: What else satisfies the same need? Online alternatives?
- **Rivalry**: Who's already doing this nearby? How aggressive?

Keep it brief per candidate — one paragraph, not an essay.

## C. Unit Economics

Use REAL numbers:
- Revenue model: per-customer, per-transaction, subscription, hourly?
- Average transaction value (from competitor pricing research)
- Estimated customers per day/month (from foot traffic + demand data)
- Gross margin (from industry benchmarks for the region)
- Fixed costs: rent (EXACT from discovery), estimated staffing, utilities, insurance
- Variable costs: inventory, supplies, marketing per customer
- Monthly breakeven: units needed, months to reach it
- Output as a table with assumptions flagged

## D. Risk Assessment Matrix

Rate each candidate 1-5 with specific reasoning:
- **Financial risk**: How much capital at stake? How long until revenue?
- **Market risk**: Is demand proven or assumed? How stable?
- **Execution risk**: Does the user have skills? Complexity level?
- **Regulatory risk**: Licensing hurdles, compliance, legal exposure?

## E. Competitive Moat Analysis

For each candidate, honestly assess:
- Location advantage (is being HERE specifically valuable?)
- Relationship advantage (user's network creates an edge?)
- Skill advantage (rare relevant skills?)
- Switching costs (would customers stick?)
- Network effects (gets better with more customers?)
- **Honest verdict**: No moat? Say so. Some businesses are commodities — fine if margins work.

## Scoring Weights

| Criterion | Weight | What it measures |
|-----------|--------|-----------------|
| Market opportunity | 25% | Gap size, demand evidence, growth trajectory |
| Financial viability | 25% | Unit economics, breakeven, capital requirements |
| Competitive position | 20% | Moat strength, rivalry, barriers to entry |
| Skill fit | 15% | Alignment with user's background and interests |
| Risk level | 15% | Inverse of total risk score |

## Comparative Analysis

For each top-3 candidate, find and cite at least one comparable real-world example:
- "A similar [business type] in [comparable city] with [similar conditions] achieved [outcome]"
- "The key factor was [specific reason] — which [does/doesn't] apply here because [evidence]"
- Draw from different geographies to show the pattern is robust, not coincidental
